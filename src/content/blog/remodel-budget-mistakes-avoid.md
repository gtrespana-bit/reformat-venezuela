## Mistake #1: Not Having Your Own Contingency Fund

### The Reality of Construction Projects

No remodel goes exactly as drawn on paper. Behind the tiles, under the floor or inside the walls you may find:
- Old pipes that need replacement
- Electrical wiring that doesn't meet current code
- Hidden moisture or leaks
- Structural unevenness requiring additional preparation

**Industry statistic:** In 80-90% of remodels, technical unforeseen issues appear. The average usually runs 10-20% of the base budget.

### How to Manage It Correctly

A transparent professional **doesn't charge you a "contingency fund" upfront**. What they should do is:
1. **Warn you** about the real possibility of unforeseen issues
2. **Recommend** that you reserve 10-15% separate from your total budget
3. **Communicate immediately** if something comes up, with a closed budget for that item before executing it

**Never sign with someone who tells you "there are no unforeseen issues here"**. That's not realism, it's naivety or bad faith.

### Real Case
A couple budgeted $8,000 for their bathroom. When they opened up the walls, they found an old asbestos cement drain pipe that required urgent replacement ($650). Since they had their reserve fund, they approved it without stress. The project finished on time and without arguments.

**Lesson:** Unforeseen issues aren't "contractor extras". They're technical realities. Having your own financial cushion gives you peace and decision-making power.

## Mistake #2: Comparing Budgets Only by Final Price

### The Global Number Trap

You receive three quotes:
- Company A: $7,500
- Company B: $9,200
- Company C: $11,000

Which do you choose? Most people just look at the number. **Big mistake.**

A professional quote isn't a number. It's a **detailed document** that should include:
- Materials (brand, model, thickness, finish)
- Labor (estimated hours, specialty)
- Municipal permits and fees
- Debris management and removal
- Final cleaning
- Written warranty
- Execution timeline

### How to Compare Correctly

Create a simple table:

| Concept | Company A | Company B | Company C |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Total price | $7,500 | $9,200 | $11,000 |
| Itemized breakdown | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Material specifications | Generic | ✅ Detailed | ✅ Detailed |
| Permits included | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Waste management | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Warranty | ❌ Verbal | ✅ 1 year | ✅ 2 years |
| Written timeline | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |

**Conclusion:** Cheap quotes that don't specify usually turn out expensive. Transparent quotes that detail protect you.

## Mistake #3: Forgetting Real Indirect Costs

### What You Should Count (And What You Shouldn't)

Many "cheap" quotes omit mandatory items to look better on paper. Then they appear as "extras".

**REAL indirect costs that should be included or clear from the start:**

| Concept | Why it's necessary | Approximate range |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Minor/major work permit | Municipal requirement | $100-$400 |
| Electrical certificate | Safety and code compliance | $150-$300 |
| Plumbing certificate | Installation guarantee | $100-$200 |
| Dumpster and debris removal | Legal and practical | $250-$500 |
| Professional final cleaning | Delivery ready to use | $150-$300 |
| Protection of non-remodeled areas | Prevents collateral damage | $100-$200 |

**What should NOT appear in a remodel quote:**
- Client's accommodation, meals or transportation expenses
- "Equivalent materials" without specifying brand
- Global items without breakdown
- "Estimated" dates without delay penalties

### How to Verify It
Always ask: *"Does this quote include permits, debris removal, certificates and final cleaning?"* If the answer is vague or negative, be suspicious.

## Mistake #4: Paying Upfront Without Clear Milestones

### The Risk of Losing Control

Paying 50% or more before starting takes away your negotiation power and protection. If the work doesn't progress or quality isn't as agreed, they've already been paid.

### Professional Payment Structure

A serious contract ties payments to **verifiable milestones**, not dates:

1. **Signing and start:** 10-15%
2. **Demolition and debris removal:** 15%
3. **Certified installations (electrical/plumbing):** 25%
4. **Cabinets and finishes installed:** 30%
5. **Final delivery, cleaning and review:** 15-20%

**Advantage:** You only pay when you see real, verifiable progress. If something doesn't meet standards, it's corrected before the next payment.

## Mistake #5: Signing Without Reading the Contract

### Phrases That Should Alert You

❌ *"Prices subject to change without notice"* → Translation: They can raise the price whenever they want
❌ *"Similar quality materials"* → Translation: They promised one thing, they'll install another
❌ *"Estimated timeline"* → Translation: There's no real deadline
❌ *"Warranty subject to evaluation"* → Translation: The warranty isn't binding

### What Your Contract Should Have

✅ Closed and definitive price (unless you request changes in writing)
✅ Technical material specifications (brand, reference, finish)
✅ Start date and delivery date with delay consequences
✅ Payment schedule tied to milestones
✅ Minimum 1-2 year written warranty
✅ Name and contact of site supervisor
✅ Resolution clause if timelines or quality aren't met

**Tip:** If they don't give you a written contract, don't sign. Trust is demonstrated with paper, not words.

## Mistake #6: Choosing by Price, Not by Real References

### How to Really Verify

"I don't know them, but they have a good price" is the phrase that generates the most regrets.

**Real verification steps:**
1. Ask for 2-3 addresses of completed projects in the last 6 months
2. Call those clients. Ask: Did they meet deadlines? Did they respect the budget? How did they solve problems?
3. Verify they have current business license and liability insurance
4. Check their real presence: office/workshop, professional website, verifiable reviews (not just generic 5 stars)

A serious company doesn't object to you verifying their work. One that evades probably has something to hide.

## Mistake #7: Not Planning Construction Logistics

### What Nobody Tells You Until It Starts

Remodeling isn't just "tear down and install". It's logistics:
- Where will you store your furniture and belongings during construction?
- How will you protect areas that aren't being remodeled?
- Who will have the keys and how will access be managed?
- Will water/electricity be available during work?

**Planning this prevents:**
- Furniture damage from dust or moisture
- Delays from lack of access or materials
- Misunderstandings with neighbors about hours or noise
- Unnecessary stress during execution

A good professional helps you organize this before starting. They don't improvise on the fly.

## Your Checklist Before Signing Any Quote

Print this and use it:

### ✅ Before Requesting Quotes
- [ ] Define exactly what I want to remodel (detailed list)
- [ ] Research real prices of materials I like
- [ ] Calculate my maximum budget (including 10-15% own contingency fund)
- [ ] Decide acceptable payment methods (never >20% upfront)

### ✅ When Receiving Quotes
- [ ] They're itemized by category, not a global number
- [ ] They specify brands, models and finishes
- [ ] They include permits, debris removal and final cleaning
- [ ] They have clear start and delivery dates
- [ ] They include written warranty
- [ ] They tie payments to verifiable milestones

### ✅ Before Signing
- [ ] Verify company license and insurance
- [ ] Call at least 2 references from recent projects
- [ ] Read the ENTIRE contract, especially price, timeline and warranty clauses
- [ ] Confirm who the supervisor will be and how to contact them
- [ ] Keep a copy signed by both parties
